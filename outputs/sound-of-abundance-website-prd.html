07 — UX PRINCIPLES
Design Philosophy
6 design philosophy statements grounded in the specific context of Sound of Abundance Church and Nairobi's Eastlands demographic.
1
POWER & PRESENCE FIRST
The site must communicate prophetic authority and Kingdom power immediately. Every design decision — from font weight to color contrast to animation speed — should reinforce the message that this is not a casual community church but a dynamic center of supernatural power and divine favor.
2
CONTENT AT THE CENTER
Sermons and teachings are the primary product of this ministry. The "Media & Sermons" section must be accessible within 1 click from anywhere on the site. YouTube video IDs are verified and embedded. Content discovery is the core user journey — not a secondary feature.
3
STORYTELLING OVER LISTING
Bios, timelines, and testimonials are crafted as narratives — not bullet-point resumes. Rev. B. Akama's story is told as a journey of prophetic calling. The timeline of the church is a story of growth in The Lord City. Even ministry descriptions read as invitations, not feature lists.
4
EASTLANDS IDENTITY
The site is deeply rooted in Nairobi's Eastlands — Kangundo Road, Saika Estate, Njiru Sub-county. Every reference to location, every service description, every imagery choice reflects the specific demographic reality of the 410,000+ working-age residents of this area. The brand is not generic international church — it is Nairobi-born, Eastlands-rooted.
5
MOBILE IS PRIMARY
Over half of all church website traffic in Kenya is mobile-initiated. The Njiru sub-county demographic is predominantly smartphone-based. Every design decision is made for mobile first. Large tap targets (48px minimum), readable text (never below 16px on inputs), aggressive asset optimization, and scannable content structure are non-negotiable.
6
ONE CLEAR ACTION PER PAGE
Each page has ONE dominant CTA. Home: Watch Live. Sermons: Watch/Discover. Give: Partner with Kingdom Business. Events: RSVP. Connect: Submit Prayer Request. This clarity serves both new visitors (they know exactly what to do) and regular congregants (they can execute tasks without searching).
